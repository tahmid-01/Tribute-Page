The Life of a Genius

Sir Isaac Newton (1643-1727) was an English physicist, mathematician, astronomer, and author who is widely recognized as one of the greatest mathematicians and scientists of all time.

Major Scientific Achievements and Contributions:

Laws of Motion (1687)

Formulated three fundamental laws of motion, forming the basis of classical mechanics, published in Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica ("The Principia").

Law of Universal Gravitation (1687)

Described the force of gravity and its universal application, published in The Principia.

Development of Calculus (mid-1660s – early 1670s)

Co-developed calculus independently, though its publication and priority disputes arose later with Leibniz.

Reflecting Telescope (1668)

Invented the first practical reflecting telescope using a parabolic mirror, improving optical clarity.

Decomposition of Light (1665–1672)

Demonstrated that white light is composed of the spectrum of colors using a prism, detailed in Opticks (1704).

Binomial Theorem Generalization (1665)

Generalized the binomial theorem for any rational exponent.

Newton's Cooling Law (1701)

Proposed a law describing the rate of heat loss in objects.

Corpuscular Theory of Light (1672)

Proposed that light is composed of particles or "corpuscles," a concept explored in Opticks.

Study of Alchemy and Chemistry

Conducted extensive, though secretive, studies on alchemy and chemical transformations.

Work on Fluid Mechanics (1687)

Formulated principles governing fluid motion in The Principia, influencing later hydrodynamics.

Academic and Professional Positions:

Chair of Mathematics at Cambridge (1669)

Appointed Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at the University of Cambridge.

Master of the Royal Mint (1699)

Reformed England's coinage and combated counterfeiting.
